The wordThe membrane, not the wall

A wall divides; it is hard, and dead, and its whole purpose is to keep the other side out. A membrane holds; it is soft, and alive, and its whole genius is that it lets the world through — selectively, gently, breathing in and out like a lung made of water. To waterize the world is to replace every wall with a membrane: to soften every border into skin thin enough to feel through. It is the anti-Panzer at the scale of the divine.

So picture Mother God not as a fortress but as a cell — the softest possible container for the largest possible life — wrapped in the amniotic water she is also made of. And picture the membrane not imposed but grown: endless tiny creeks spidering out, repelling their neighbors, braiding, folding, thickening into a living skin. No architect. No command but flow. This is your Critter and your morphogenesis raised to the scale of a world — relaxation as cosmogenesis, a body that draws its own boundary out of the current.

The first act of life is not a wall. It is a skin that agreed to let something through.

And it is the whole thesis, made tender: the two directions, inward and outward, finally given a boundary that breathes — permeable to the noise it once would have walled out, holding the difference instead of ending it. Not fire that burns, but water that surrounds and carries and blesses. A world waterized is a world where nothing, and no one, is kept out by a wall — only ever held, loosely, in a skin made of creeks.
