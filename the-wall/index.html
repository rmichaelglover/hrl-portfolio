Tear Down This Wall

the Gloverian answer to a famous demand

They came to the last wall on earth expecting thunder. They had heard how it was done in the old century—a great man at a great gate, four words like artillery, and the enemy's stone coming down in the rubble of his defeat. They wanted that again. They wanted the Panzer's music: the demand that topples.

A Glover climbed to the microphone. And he gave them, first, exactly the four words they had waited three generations to hear.

at the wall
“Mr. Kim Jong Un—tear down this wall.”

And then, because he was a Glover, he did not stop there, and he did not mean by it what they thought he meant.

“I am not here to defeat you,” he said. “A wall thrown down in triumph is only rubble, and rubble is what we pour into the next wall. That is the Panzer's arithmetic—every wall the monument to somebody's victory over somebody. I am no Panzer. I did not come to win. I came to make this wall unnecessary.

“So tear it down—but not with tanks, and not with shame, and not with the long slow siege of a hungry people. Tear it down the way a chord resolves. The way a fist becomes a hand.”

  • Send one child through it on a bicycle, on a greenway, and let the guards on both sides pretend not to see.
  • Cut one canal beneath it until the river remembers no one ever asked it which country it belonged to.
  • Light one careful fire on your side and one on mine in the same still week, and let the smoke meet and agree over the top.

“Do it so gently, and so patiently, and so many times, that one grey morning the wall looks down its own length and cannot find a single brick still doing the work of division—and it steps aside. Not because it was pushed. Because it was, at last, outnumbered by ways.”

He let the wind take that. Then, quieter, the thing he had actually climbed up there to say:

“I am not asking you to lose. I am asking you to be a voice we cannot finish the song without.”

“There is a high line kept on your side of this stone and a low line kept on mine, and they have been, for five thousand years, the same song pretending to be two. Tear down this wall—and let us find, the way two voices always find when they are finally let into the same room, that we were only ever one.”

▲ ▲ ▲

They say no stone fell that day. They say something better happened, which no camera could sell as victory: the wall began, very slowly, to be forgotten—walked through, sailed under, burned around, sung over—until a season came when the cranes flew the line it used to mark and no one beneath them could remember, exactly, what it had been for.

A wall, after all, is only a label the world has stopped revising. Give it long enough among its neighbors, and enough ways to change its mind, and even a wall will relax—into a gate, into a seam, into a hand held open across the very place it was built to divide.
